We can distinguish between 4 classes of signals:

  1. Finite-length
  2. Finite-support
  3. Periodic
  4. Infinite-length

This first section covers the discrete fourier transform (DFT), which is concerned with finite-length signals

Finite-length signals can be written in sequence form as \(x[n], n = 0,1,1,...,N-1\) or equivalently as vectors in the domain of complex numbers \(\mathbb{C}^N\)

We can use the fourier basis instead of the standard basis in \(\mathbb{C}^N\) to look at any finite-length sequence. The fourier basis is an orthogonal basis and a basis vector of index \(k\) is denoted by the following:

\[\mathbf{w}^{(k)} = e^{\mathrm{j}\frac{2\pi}{N}nk}\]

Proof of orthogonality: \[\begin{align}\\ \langle\mathbf{w}^{(k)},\mathbf{w}^{(h)}\rangle &= \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}(e^{-\mathrm{j}\frac{2\pi}{N}nk})^*e^{\mathrm{j}\frac{2\pi}{N}nh}\\ &=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}e^{\mathrm{j}\frac{2\pi}{N}(h-k)n}\\ &=\begin{cases} N\quad&\text{ for }h=k \\ \frac{1-e^{\mathrm{j}2\pi(h-k)}}{1-e^{\mathrm{j}\frac{2\pi}{N}(h-k)}}=0\quad&\text{ otherwise} \end{cases}\quad \end{align}\]

This last part follows due to the fact that for \(h \neq k\): \(\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}a^n=\frac{1-a^N}{1-a}\) with \(h,k \in \mathbb{Z}\) (the set of integers), so the term in the numerator will always be \(0\)

So, because the Fourier basis is a basis of the vector space \(\mathbb{C}^N\) and thus spans all possible linear combinations in \(\mathbb{C}^N\), we can project any finite-length signal (represented by a vector in \(\mathbb{C}^N\)) onto this basis and obtain a unique representation of the signal in the so-called "frequency" domain.

Due to Euler's formula, \(\mathbf{w}^{(k)} = e^{\mathrm{j}\frac{2\pi}{N}nk} = cos(\frac{2\pi}{N}nk) + \mathrm{j}sin(\frac{2\pi}{N}nk)\)

Each of these basis vectors can thus be seen as real and complex sinusoids. A signal expressed as a linear combination of Fourier basis vectors is then just the superposition of these sinusoids.

For (a simple) example, consider the signal \(x[n] = 3cos(\frac{2\pi}{32}n + \frac{\pi}{3})\) in the time domain with 32 discrete time intervals:

We can project this signal vector onto the Fourier basis in order to obtain a basis vector. First, we need to construct a change of basis matrix \(U\):

\[U = \begin{bmatrix} e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=0, n=0} & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=1, n=0} & \cdots & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=31, n=0} \\[0.3em] e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=0, n=1} & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=1, n=1} & \cdots & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=31, n=1} \\[0.3em] \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\[0.3em] e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=0, n=31} & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=1, n=31} & \cdots & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{2\pi}{32}nk}\big|_{k=31, n=31} \\[0.3em] \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\[0.3em] 1 & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{\pi}{16}} & \cdots & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{62\pi}{32}} \\[0.3em] \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\[0.3em] 1 & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{62\pi}{32}} & \cdots & e^{\mathrm{-j}\frac{1922\pi}{32}} \\[0.3em] \end{bmatrix} \]

We can then obtain the DFT basis vector using the following transformation: \(X = Ux\) where \(x \in \mathbb{C}^N\)

We can then talk about the magnitude and phase of each of these basis vectors. The magnitude (the length) of a particular Fourier basis vector can be thought of as representing the "energy" at that particular frequency. In this example, at \(\mathbf{w}^{(1)}\) the frequency is \(\frac{2\pi}{32}\) and at \(\mathbf{w}^{(31)}\) the frequency is \(\frac{62\pi}{32}\). The first vector moves counterclockwise, and the second clockwise, both moving by the same absolute degree interval. We see from the figure below that both of these vectors contribute equally to the signal (and in fact are the only ones to do so). The phase tells us where these vectors "start" as they work their way around the circle in the complex plane. In this example, both vectors "start" opposite one another with respect to the real axis.

We see that a linear combination of the basis vectors \(\mathbf{w}^{(1)}\) and \(\mathbf{w}^{(31)}\) with magnitudes of \(16\) and angles of \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) and \(-\frac{\pi}{3}\) respectively is needed to represent this signal. How can we get back to the time domain represenation of the signal?

We can prove that \(\vec{x} = \langle\vec{u_0}, \vec{x}\rangle\vec{u_0} + \cdots + \langle\vec{u_{31}}, \vec{x}\rangle\vec{u_{31}}\) given an orthonormal basis \(\vec{u_0},\cdots,\vec{u_{31}}\) of \(\mathbb{C}^N\). However, the Fourier basis that we have is not an orthonormal basis, as the basis vectors are not unit vectors. To convert the vectors into unit vectors, we have to divide them by \(\sqrt N\) to obtain \(u_k = \frac{\mathbf{w}^{(k)}}{\sqrt N}\). We can then check that \(\|u_k\| = \sqrt{\langle\frac{\mathbf{w}^{(k)}}{\sqrt N},\frac{\mathbf{w}^{(k)}}{\sqrt N}\rangle} = 1\) (satisfying the definition of a unit vector).

The above relation can then be simplified to: \(\vec{x} = \frac{1}{N}\langle\mathbf{w}^{(0)}, \vec{x}\rangle\mathbf{w}^{(0)} + \cdots + \frac{1}{N}\langle\mathbf{w}^{(31)}, \vec{x}\rangle\mathbf{w}^{(31)} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}X_k\mathbf{w}^{(k)}\) where \(X_k\) is the k-th DFT coefficient. We can sum these sinusoids in the time domain to get back the original signal:

If we recall the phase of these two vectors, we can verify that as they move around the circle their imaginary parts will cancel each other out. More complex examples may necessitate contributions from many (or all) Fourier basis vectors and may accrue imaginary components as the weighted basis vectors are summed. Regardless of the complexity of the signal, because it lies in the time domain (and thus composed only of real numbers), these contributions will ultimately result in no imaginary components.
